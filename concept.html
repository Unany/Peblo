Peblos Game ConceptLast updated: 26-07-2021

Every game project, no matter the scale, needs careful planning. One of the main reasons for this is because ideas change over time.

Jumping right into a game without a plan often deviates from its original idea. Having a plan helps avoid deviation while at the same time helps transmit your ideas to your team better.

Almost every game enthusiast and aspiring artist at some point wants to turn their own ideas into a game. Making games isn’t necessarily hard, but it’s a rather tedious process.

The Core

The Three Points

We have 8 seconds to capture someone's attention. Whilst you're reading this. Those second's have already gone.

That's why short and punchy descriptions are key. This is where the three point approach comes in. If you head to the steam store page right now and click on a top seller. The majority of the time it will have three sentences in it's description.

We need to give the consumer the gist of the game, an overview of the plot and the tonality. Below is Peblos:

"If you could fight fate. Would you? Find out in this top-down action-adventure. Gather your courage and propogate the land with hope!""

The game's style

For me the game's style has a lot to do with fond memories. I find the time's when nothing is happening, or i'm not forcing it are the times my inspiration comes.

In the case of Peblo, I dreamt of an autumn scene, walking aross the british countryside. From that inspiration I scour the web (mainly pinterest) for images that allow me to daydream scenes. It's from this daydreaming that the rest of the pieces fall together.



I then take the images and come up with a colour palette for the game. Personally, I use adobe colour wheel as it's free and allows for accompanying



Lastly, I needed to ask myself "What type of textures do I like". This was relatively easy for me, throughout my life i've adored studio ghilbi. If you've played Peblo, you'll see that the art style partially mimics this. However, In order to do this we need to take into account several things.

1. It's a handpainted style
2. The textures are simple, not overcomplicated.
3. The textures are sometimes overlayed by another texture to create depth. E.G. Moss on a rock


The game's setting

I wanted the world to be something personal, something which I have fond memories of, so that I can build the world with evocative emotion.

WHen I was younger I went on a camping trip to the lake district with my Dad and brother. I fell in love with the lake district. Rich, fond emotions are always their when I think about itself. The decision was easy! Why not make the world out of the UK, the lake district behind the setting for Peblo. Now obviously we can't copy it 1 to 1. The scale of the world would be unmangeable for a 1 person scene, so I came up with a better idea. Trace the map of the lake district.



This is a perfect stage to block out a basic world, where the rivers go, the mountains and any other features. Just remember the world WILL Change, don't spend ages doing it. I used DungeonFog for this and just blocked it out with basic colours.

Characters

Peblo is a cultivation of year's worth of imagining. I wanted a character that grew along with whatever story the game had. He had to be hollow to start with, emotionless, talentless and realise his potential through tasks.

Peblo is the embodiment of that, a clay doll brought to life.

Below are some iterations of Peblo, made in adobe illustrator.




In order to give a character life, I like to model it out of clay. It gives me perspective, allows me to feel like Peblo's real.

Below is Peblo, in his 3D clay glory!



An overview of the story

1.Why i didnt want to plan the story to start with 2.Minimum story I'm not a natural writer. I come up with plot's in my head, but developign it and world-building I struggle with. Therefore, it's easier for me to develop the art, world and characters before I make the story. My process is as follows:

1. Have a moment of inspiration
2. Explain it to my girlfriend to see if she likes it as a non-gamer
3. Look for art inspiration online, that matches what I want to do (I'm not artistic, so its either this or stick figures!)
4. Develop my world
5. Develop my characters
6. The story then just comes to me

Music

Just like art, I'm inept at music. That's a huge problem in a video game as a good soundtrack can make or break a game. Luckily technology has come leaps and bounds and AI generated music is available from companys such as "AIVA".jpeg

I use AIVA for all my concept music. If I had a budget for music in my game, I would definitely consider music to be one of the most important elements to invest in. But because i'm an indie dev with no budget, I compromised with AI generated music.
